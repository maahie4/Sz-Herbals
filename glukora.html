Understanding the condition

What actually happens
when you have diabetes?

Diabetes is not simply "too much sugar." It is fundamentally a breakdown in your body's ability to produce or use insulin — the hormone your pancreas makes to convert glucose into energy your cells can use.

When the pancreas is damaged, inflamed, or exhausted, it stops producing enough insulin. Without insulin, glucose floods the bloodstream — leading to the symptoms and long-term damage we know as diabetes.

1

You eat food containing carbohydrates

Your digestive system breaks food down into glucose, which enters your bloodstream and causes blood sugar to rise.

2

The pancreas senses the rise in blood sugar

Specialised cells in the pancreas called beta cells — housed in clusters called Islets of Langerhans — are triggered to produce insulin.

3

Insulin acts as the "key" to your cells

Insulin unlocks your body's cells, allowing glucose to enter and be converted to energy. Blood sugar then drops back to normal.

4

In diabetes, this system breaks down

The pancreas cannot produce enough insulin (Type 2 / worn-out beta cells) or produces none at all (Type 1). Glucose stays trapped in the blood — causing damage to every organ over time.
